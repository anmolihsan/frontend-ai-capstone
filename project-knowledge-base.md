## 1. Writing Style Guide

### Voice & Tone
- Professional yet approachable — write as a knowledgeable peer, not a textbook.
- Conversational where appropriate (introductions, transitions), formal where it matters (findings, conclusions, technical claims).
- Confident but not overstated — avoid hedging chains ("it could perhaps possibly suggest…").

### Structure
- Bullet points over dense paragraphs wherever a list of items, steps, or comparisons is involved.
- Paragraphs reserved for argument-building, narrative, or nuanced explanation that doesn't break cleanly into bullets.
- Use headings and subheadings to make long documents scannable.
- Lead with the point, then support it — avoid burying conclusions at the end of long paragraphs.

### Language Conventions
- **British English throughout**: organise (not organize), colour (not color), analyse (not analyze), programme (not program, except software), centre (not center), -ise/-isation endings.
- Avoid unnecessary jargon; when technical terms are required, briefly define them on first use unless the audience is clearly specialist.
- Active voice preferred over passive, except in methods/results sections where passive is conventional (e.g. "the survey was distributed to…").

### Citations & Evidence
- Claims of fact, statistics, or established findings should be evidence-based and cited.
- Default citation style: **IEEE** for Software Engineering/technical work unless a specific conference/journal specifies otherwise (e.g. APA, ACM).
- Flag clearly when a claim is an inference or opinion rather than a cited fact.

## 4. Formatting Defaults for Deliverables

- **Assignments / reports**: Word doc (.docx) with headings, page numbers, and a table of contents for anything over ~5 pages.
- **Presentations**: slide deck (.pptx), one idea per slide, minimal text, visuals over paragraphs.
- **Study notes / roadmaps**: Markdown, so they're easy to version, search, and reuse across tools.
- **Data-heavy work**: spreadsheet (.xlsx) with clearly labelled sheets/tabs rather than dense single-sheet layouts.

---

## 5. Things to Avoid

- Long unbroken paragraphs where a list would be clearer.
- American spellings creeping into British English documents.
- Unsupported factual claims presented without a citation or caveat.
- Overly casual tone in submission-ready academic material (capstone report, conference abstract).
